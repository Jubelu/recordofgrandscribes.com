VITA GLORIOSISSIMI LUDOVICI FRANCORUM REGIS ILLUSTRISSIMI INCIPIT FELICITER.

CAPUT PRIMUM.

I. Quam strenuus in adolescentia fuerit, et quanta strenuitate fortissimum regem Anglorum Willelmum Rufum paternum regnum turbantem repulerit.

1 Gloriosus igitur et famosus rex Francorum Ludovicus regis magnifici Philippi filius, primaevae flore aetatis fere adhuc duodennis, seu tredennis, elegans et formosus, tanta morum probabilium venerabili industria, tanta amoenissimi corporis proceritate proficiebat, ut et sceptris futuris re ipsa amplificationem honorificam incunctanter promitteret, et Ecclesiarum et pauperum tuitioni spem votivam generaret. Altus puerulus, antiqua regum Caroli Magni et aliorum excellentiorum hoc ipsum testamentis imperialibus testificantium consuetudine, apud Sanctum Dionysium tanta et quasi nativa dulcedine ipsis sanctis martyribus suisque adhaesit, usque adeo ut innatam a puero eorum Ecclesiae amicitiam toto tempore vitae suae multa liberalitate et honorificentia continuaret, et in fine summe post Deum sperans ab eis seipsum et corpore et anima, ut, si fieri posset, ibidem monachus efficeretur, devotissime deliberando liberando contraderet.

>2 Sane praefata aetate, animo juvenili vigere maturabat virtus nativa impatiens venationum, et ludicrorum puerilium, quibus aetas hujusmodi lascivire et arma dediscere consuescit. Dumque multorum regni optimatum et egregie magnanimi regis Anglorum Guillelmi, magnanimioris Guillelmi regis filii Anglorum domitoris infestatione agitatur, robur probitatis vaporat, exercitio virtus arridet, inertiam removet, prudentiae oculum aperit, otium dissolvit, sollicitudinem accelerat. Guillelmus siquidem rex Anglorum, usui militiae aptus, laudis avarus famaeque petitor, cum, exhaeredato majore natu Roberto fratre suo, patri Guillelmo feliciter successisset, et post ejusdem fratris sui Hierosolymam profectionem ducatum Northmanniae obtinuisset sicut ejusdem Northmanniae ducatus se porrigit marchiis regni collimitans, quibuscunque poterat modis famosum juvenem nitebatur impugnare.

3 Similiter et dissimiliter inter eos certabatur, similiter cum neuter cederet, dissimiliter cum ille maturus, iste juvenculus: ille opulentus et Anglorum thesaurorum profusor, mirabilisque militum mercator et solidator; iste peculii expers patri, qui beneficiis regni utebatur, parcendo, sola bonae indolis industria militiam cogebat, audacter resistebat. Videres juvenem celerrimum modo Bituricensium, modo Arvernorum, modo Burgundionum militari manu transvolare fines, nec idcirco tardius, si ei ignotescat, Vilcassinum regredi, et cum trecentis aut quingentis militibus praefato regi Guillelmo cum 10 millibus fortissime refragari, et, ut dubius se habet belli eventus, modo cedere, modo fugare.

4 Talibus utrobique multi intercipiebantur congressionibus, quorum famosus juvenis, et sui, cum plures alios, tum comitem Simonem nobilem virum, Gislebertum de Aquila nobilem, et Angliae et Northmanniae aeque illustrem baronem, Paganum de Gisortio, qui castrum idem primo munivit Rex e contrario Angliae strenuum et nobilem comitem Matthaeum Bellimontensem, illustrem et magni nominis baronem Simonem de Monteforti, dominum Montis-Gai Paganum captos tenuerunt. Verum Angliae captos ad redemptionem celerem militaris stipendii acceleravit anxietas; Francorum vero longa diuturni carceris maceravit prolixitas, nec ullo modo evinculari potuerunt, donec suscepta ejusdem regis Angliae militia, hominio obligati regnum et regem impugnare et turbare jurejurando firmaverunt.

5 Dicebatur equidem vulgo regem illum superbum et impetuosum aspirare ad regnum Francorum, quia famosus juvenis unicus patri erat de nobilissima conjuge Roberti Flandrensis comitis sorore ( Berta ). Qui enim duo supererant, Philippus et Florus, de super ducta Andegavensi comitissa Bertrada geniti erant, nec illorum appretiabatur successionem, si unicum primum decedere quocunque infortunio contingeret. Verum, quia nec fas nec naturale est Francos Anglis, imo Anglos Francis subjici, spem repulsivam rei delusit eventus. Nam, cum per triennium aut eo amplius haec insania se et suos exagitasset, nec per Anglos, nec per Francos hominio obligatus proficiendo voluntati suae satisfacere valeret, subsedit. Cumque in Angliam transfretasset, lasciviae et animi desideriis deditus, cum quadam die in Nova silva venationibus insisteret, subito inopinata sagitta percussus interiit ( an. 1100).

6 Divinatum est virum divina ultione percussum, assumpto veritatis argumento, eo quod pauperum exstiterat intolerabilis oppressor, Ecclesiarum crudelis exactor, et si quando episcopi vel praelati decederent, irreverentissimus retentor et dissipator. Imponebatur a quibusdam cuidam nobilissimo viro Galterio Tirello quod eum sagitta perfoderat Quem cum nec timeret, nec speraret, jurejurando saepius audivimus, et quasi sacrosanctum asserere quod ea die nec in in eam partem silvae in qua rex venabatur venerit, nec eum in silva omnino viderit. Unde constat tantam tam subito tanti divina potentia in favillam evanuisse insaniam, ut qui alios supervacanee inquietabat, gravius infinite inquietetur, et qui omnia appetebat, inglorius omnibus exuatur. Deo enim, qui baltheum regum discingit, regna et regnorum jura subjiciuntur.

7 Successit eidem Guillelmo quam celeriter in regno frater minor natu (quoniam Robertus major in illa magna expeditione sancti sepulcri agebat) vir prudentissimus Henricus, cujus tam admiranda quam praedicanda animi et corporis strenuitas et scientia gratam offerrent materiam. Sed nil nostra refert, nisi si aliquid incidenter nostris convertibile, aliquando nos oporteat, sicut et de regno Lotharingorum, summatim praelibare. Francorum enim, non Anglorum gesta quodam scripto memoriae mandare proposuimus.

HERE BEGINS, IN JOY, THE LIFE OF THE MOST GLORIOUS LOUIS, THE MOST ILLUSTROUS KING OF THE FRANKS

 

FIRST CHAPTER

How vigorous he was in his youth, and with what great energy he repelled William Rufus, the most powerful king of the English, when he was disturbing his father’s kingdom.  

1 Thus Louis, the glorious and renowned king of the Franks, son of the magnificent King Philip, while still in the very flower of early youth—scarcely twelve or thirteen years old—handsome and well-formed, advanced with such venerable diligence in commendable conduct, and with such pleasing stature of body, that he unmistakably promised in reality an honorable enlargement of the sceptres he was destined to wield, and inspired hopeful expectation for the protection of the Churches and of the poor. While still a tall little boy, following the ancient custom of kings descending from Charlemagne and other illustrious rulers who bore witness to this very thing in imperial charters, he attached himself to Saint-Denis with such great and almost innate sweetness toward the holy martyrs there, that he continued throughout his whole life the friendship for their Church which he had formed in childhood, showing it much generosity and honor; and at the end, placing supreme hope after God in them, he most devoutly resolved to entrust and commend himself to them both in body and soul, even to the point—if it could be done—of becoming a monk there.

2 Indeed, at the aforesaid age, native virtue ripened early with youthful vigor, impatient of hunting and childish games, by which such an age is accustomed to indulge in wantonness and to unlearn the use of arms. And while he was harassed by the attacks of many of the leading men of the kingdom and of William, the notably magnanimous king of the English—son of the still more magnanimous King William, conqueror of the English—his moral strength burned hotter, virtue smiled upon exercise, sloth was driven away, the eye of prudence was opened, leisure was broken, and diligence quickened. For William, king of the English, fit for the practice of war, greedy for praise and eager for fame, after disinheriting his elder brother Robert and succeeding happily to his father William, and after his brother’s departure for Jerusalem having obtained the duchy of Normandy, which borders the marches of the kingdom of France along its whole extent, strove by every means he could to attack the famous youth.

3 Thus they contended both similarly and differently: similarly, since neither yielded; differently, since one was mature, the other youthful; one wealthy, lavish with the treasures of the English, a marvelous buyer and payer of soldiers; the other lacking private resources, with a father who governed the kingdom sparingly, compelled military service solely by the energy of his good character, yet resisted boldly. You might see the swift young prince now flying with armed force across the borders of the Bituriges, now of the Auvergne, now of Burgundy; nor therefore more slowly, if the situation demanded it, returning to the Vexin, and with three hundred or five hundred knights most bravely resisting the aforesaid King William with ten thousand men—now yielding, now putting him to flight, as the uncertain outcome of war allowed.

4 In these frequent encounters on both sides many were captured. Among those taken by the famous youth and his men were Simon the count, a noble man; Gilbert of Aquila, noble and equally illustrious in England and Normandy; and Pagan of Gisors, who had first fortified that castle. Conversely, the king of England captured the valiant and noble Count Matthew of Beaumont, the illustrious and renowned baron Simon of Montfort, lord of Montjay, and Pagan. The English captives were quickly ransomed, urgency over military pay hastening their release; but the French were worn down by the long prolongation of harsh imprisonment, and could not be freed in any way until, having entered the military service of the king of England and bound by homage, they swore an oath to attack and disturb the kingdom and king of France.

5 It was commonly said that this proud and impetuous king aspired to the kingdom of France, because the famous youth was his father’s only son by a most noble wife, Bertha, sister of Robert, count of Flanders. For the two others who survived, Philip and Florus, were born of Bertrada, countess of Anjou, whom the king had taken later; and William did not value their claim to succession, if by any misfortune the first and only son should die. But since it is neither lawful nor natural that the Franks be subjected to the English—or rather, the English to the Franks—the outcome mocked and repelled this hope. For when over three years or more this madness had tormented him and his followers, and he could not satisfy his will by progress either through English or French bound to him by homage, he desisted. And when he had crossed to England and given himself over to luxury and the desires of his spirit, one day while engaged in hunting in the New Forest, he was suddenly struck by an unexpected arrow and perished (in the year 1100).

6 It was judged that the man had been struck by divine vengeance, as a clear proof of truth, because he had been an intolerable oppressor of the poor, a cruel extorter from the Churches, and—whenever bishops or prelates died—a most irreverent retainer and squanderer of their revenues. Some accused a most noble man, Walter Tirel, of piercing him with the arrow; yet him we have often heard swear solemnly, without fear or hope of gain, and assert as though sacred truth, that on that day he neither came to that part of the forest where the king was hunting, nor saw him in the forest at all. Thus it is evident that so great a madness vanished so suddenly into ashes by divine power, that he who needlessly troubled others was himself troubled infinitely more severely, and he who grasped at everything was stripped of all in disgrace. For to God, who unbuckles the belt of kings, kingdoms and the rights of kingdoms are subject.

7 William was succeeded with great speed in the kingdom by his younger brother Henry (since Robert the elder was then engaged in that great expedition to the Holy Sepulchre), a most prudent man, whose strength and knowledge of mind and body were as admirable as they were worthy of praise. But this does not concern us, except insofar as something incidentally relevant to our subject may require brief mention, as also with the kingdom of Lotharingia. For we have proposed to commit to memory in writing the deeds of the Franks, not of the English.
